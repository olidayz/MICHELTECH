# Voiceover

## When VO Works

Voiceover is powerful when it adds something the image can't. Use it for:

### Narration / Information
- Explaining concepts the image can't show (history, backstory)
- Guiding the viewer through complex information
- "Here is how we got here" exposition

### Internal Monologue
- Access to a character's private thoughts
- Creates intimacy even with limited visual
- "The Unreliable Narrator" — what they say vs. what we see

### Commentary / Essay
- Documentaries, video essays, Op-Docs
- The voice guides interpretation of the images
- Makes the viewer feel they're being told a story

### Rhythm / Texture
- The voice as a musical element (beat, rhythm, texture)
- More about sound than information
- "Just tell me about your day" talking heads that are ASMR-adjacent

## When VO Is a Crutch

VO is often a shortcut when the director didn't figure out how to show it visually. Ask:

- **Can the image tell this?** If yes, cut the VO.
- **Is the VO explaining what we can already see?** Probably cut it.
- **Is the VO more interesting than the image?** Then the image is the problem.

## VO Styles

### Conversational / Natural
- "Just talking" feel
- How people actually speak to friends
- Intimate, relatable, low-stakes
- Best for: documentaries, personal stories, ads that feel like advice

### Authoritative / Direct
- "Here's the truth" energy
- Clear, confident, knows what they're talking with
- Best for: explainers, tutorials, trust-based content

### Whispered / Intimate
- "This is just for you" feeling
- Secrets, vulnerability, closeness
- Best for: emotional moments, horror, ASMR-adjacent

### Performed / Theatrical
- Clearly "performing" — not natural
- Best for: period pieces, stylized content, specific character voices
- Risk: can feel pretentious if not earned

### Unreliable Narrator
- What they say contradicts what we see
- Misdirects, deceives, or reveals
- Best for: mystery, thriller, satire

## Writing VO

### Rules
1. **Write how people speak.** Contractions. Fragment sentences. Natural rhythm.
2. **Short is better.** Cut every word that doesn't earn its place.
3. **Don't describe what we see.** "We see a house" — no. "The house I grew up in" — yes.
4. **Subtext matters.** What they DON'T say is often more powerful.
5. **Match the character's voice.** Not everyone sounds the same.

### Example
**Bad VO:**
"As I walked into the old house, I noticed it was very old and hadn't been lived in for a long time. Dust covered everything. I remembered being here as a child."

**Good VO:**
"I haven't been here since I was ten. The dust knows."

## AI Voice Tools

For generating voiceover:

### ElevenLabs
- Best for: natural voices, emotional range, cloning
- Strengths: emotion control, language support, dubbing
- Best for: character voices, high-quality narration

### Suno
- Best for: music + vocals
- Strengths: can generate singing, integrated with music

### Udio
- Best for: music + vocals
- Similar to Suno, different models

### Generic TTS
- Avoid for production — can sound robotic
- Acceptable for quick tests/concepts only

### Voice Direction for AI
- Specify: "warm and intimate," "authoritative but kind," "slight British accent"
- Specify: "fast pace, excited" vs "slow, measured"
- Specify: age, gender presentation, emotional register

## VO in the Production Plan

For each section with VO, note:
```
VO: [Content summary]
Style: [Conversational/Authoritative/Whispered/etc.]
Character voice: [If applicable]
Timing: [Duration, sync points]
Music/Sound: [Does VO sit over music? Under music? By itself?]
```

**Example:**
```
VO: Character remembers her mother making bread as a child
Style: Whispered, intimate
Character: Woman, 40s, Southern accent
Timing: 12 seconds, starts at SHOT 03, ends during SHOT 04
Music/Sound: Under music, let VO breathe, fade music during SHOT 04
```
