# Sound Design

## Sound Is Half the Experience
Close your eyes during a horror movie — it's the sound that scares you. Remove the score from an emotional scene — it falls flat. Sound is not post-production decoration. It's a storytelling tool as powerful as the image.

## Types of Sound

### Diegetic Sound
Sounds that exist within the story world. Characters can hear them.
- Dialogue, footsteps, doors, traffic, rain on a window, a phone ringing
- Grounds the audience in the scene
- Establishes location and atmosphere

### Non-Diegetic Sound
Sounds that exist outside the story world. Characters can't hear them.
- Score/music, narrator voiceover, sound effects for emphasis
- Guides emotion, provides commentary, creates tension

### Meta-Diegetic
Sound representing a character's internal experience.
- Ringing ears after an explosion, heartbeat during fear, muffled sound underwater
- Puts the audience inside the character's perception

## Sound as Emotion

| Sound Element | Emotional Effect |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Silence | Tension, dread, isolation, calm, aftermath |
| Low frequency drone | Unease, dread, power, something approaching |
| High frequency tone | Anxiety, alertness, pain, disorientation |
| Rhythmic pulse | Urgency, heartbeat, countdown, mechanical |
| Nature ambient | Peace, openness, freedom, vulnerability |
| Urban ambient | Energy, anonymity, overwhelm, life |
| Reverb/echo | Space, emptiness, memory, cathedral, void |
| Distortion | Anger, breakdown, corruption, overload |
| Sudden silence after noise | Impact, shock, realization, death |
| Single clear sound in silence | Focus, importance, solitude |

## Sound Layering

A rich soundscape has layers:

1. **Bed/Ambient:** The constant background. Sets the location. City hum, forest ambience, room tone, wind.
2. **Sound Effects (SFX):** Specific sounds tied to actions. Footsteps, doors, impacts, object interactions.
3. **Foley:** Detailed human sounds. Clothing rustle, breath, finger taps, eating. Intimacy and realism.
4. **Music/Score:** Emotional driver. Can dominate or sit underneath.
5. **Dialogue/VO:** Human voice. Usually takes priority in the mix.

Not every layer is present in every scene. Choosing what to OMIT is as powerful as what you include.

## Silence as Design

Silence is not the absence of sound design — it IS sound design.
- A moment of silence before a loud event amplifies the impact
- Silence in conversation = tension, subtext, discomfort
- Removing all ambient sound = unreality, dream, isolation
- The audience will lean in during silence

## Sound Transitions

- **Sound bridge:** Audio from next scene starts before the visual cut (L-cut). Smooth, anticipatory.
- **Hard cut to silence:** Shock, finality, death, realization.
- **Sound match:** Similar sounds bridge different scenes (typing → rain, heartbeat → drumbeat).
- **Gradual fade:** Scene ending, time passing, drifting away.
- **Rising tone/build → cut:** Tension released or transferred.

## For AI Filmmaking

Sound is where AI filmmakers often fail — all effort goes into visuals. But sound is what makes AI video feel like a real film vs. a tech demo.

- **Plan sound during storyboarding.** Note sound direction for each shot.
- **Music is structural.** The beat drop, the silence, the build — these should align with visual beats.
- **Use AI audio tools** for music generation and voice, but layer with real sound effects when possible.
- **Room tone matters.** Even AI-generated scenes feel more real with appropriate ambient sound.

## Sound Notes Format

For each shot/scene in the production plan, include:
```
SOUND: [Ambient bed] + [Key SFX] + [Music note] + [VO if any]
Example: "Quiet room tone, clock ticking, faint traffic outside | SFX: chair creak, coffee cup placed on table | Music: none — silence is the score | VO: none"
```
